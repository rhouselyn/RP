## 1. Abstract

Metal halide perovskite solar cells (PSCs) have witnessed a meteoric rise in power conversion efficiency (PCE), now exceeding 26% in single-junction devices, yet their long-term operational stability remains the principal bottleneck hindering commercial deployment. The intrinsic instability of the perovskite lattice, exacerbated by ionic defects concentrated at grain boundaries and charge-transport interfaces, drives degradation under illumination, heat, and moisture. This research proposal addresses these degradation pathways through an integrated strategy combining defect engineering and interface modification. By designing multifunctional passivation molecules, constructing low-dimensional/3D heterostructures, and unravelling degradation mechanisms via operando characterization, this work targets PSCs delivering PCE above 25% with T80 lifetimes exceeding 5,000 hours under continuous one-sun illumination. The outcomes will provide both mechanistic understanding and a scalable materials platform contributing to the commercial viability of perovskite photovoltaics.

## 2. Background and Motivation

Lead halide perovskites (LHPs), with the general formula ABX3, have emerged as a leading next-generation photovoltaic technology owing to their exceptional optoelectronic properties — including high absorption coefficients, long carrier diffusion lengths, tunable bandgaps, and defect-tolerant electronic structures. Within just over a decade, the certified PCE of single-junction PSCs has surged from 3.8% to over 26%, approaching the Shockley–Queisser limit.

Despite this remarkable progress, the translation of PSCs from laboratory to market is impeded by their limited long-term stability. Unlike mature photovoltaic technologies such as crystalline silicon, which routinely exceed 25-year operational lifetimes, state-of-the-art PSCs typically degrade substantially within hundreds to thousands of hours under operational stress. The origins of instability are multifaceted, encompassing moisture-induced decomposition, thermal degradation, photo-induced phase segregation, ion migration, and interfacial reactions with charge-transport layers.

A unifying theme underlying many of these degradation pathways is the presence of crystallographic defects — particularly undercoordinated Pb2+ ions, iodide vacancies, and Pb–I antisite defects — that concentrate at grain boundaries (GBs) and surfaces. These defects not only act as non-radiative recombination centres that limit open-circuit voltage (Voc) but also serve as initiation sites for moisture ingress and ion migration. Ion migration, in particular, manifests as current–voltage hysteresis, phase segregation in mixed-halide compositions, and accelerated degradation under bias and illumination. Furthermore, the interfaces between the perovskite absorber and adjacent charge-transport layers (CTLs) — such as TiO2, SnO2, NiOx, and spiro-OMeTAD — are chemically and energetically heterogeneous regions where interfacial reactions, band misalignment, and defect accumulation further compromise stability.

Defect engineering and interface modification have therefore become central research themes. Strategies such as compositional engineering (mixed-cation FA/MA/Cs and mixed-halide I/Br formulations), additive engineering (ionic liquids, Lewis bases and acids), surface passivation (bulky organic ammonium salts forming 2D perovskites), and CTL interfacial modification have each demonstrated meaningful, albeit partial, stability improvements. However, these approaches are often pursued in isolation, and a holistic understanding of how defect–interface coupling governs long-term degradation remains elusive. This proposal seeks to close that gap.

## 3. Research Objectives

This proposal aims to develop a coherent, integrated framework linking defect chemistry at buried interfaces to macroscopic device stability, and to exploit this understanding to fabricate highly efficient and durable PSCs. The specific objectives are:

1. **Identify and quantify the dominant ionic and electronic defects** at the perovskite/CTL buried interfaces and grain boundaries, and establish their correlation with degradation kinetics under operational stress.
2. **Design and synthesize multifunctional passivation molecules** capable of simultaneously neutralizing multiple defect types (Pb2+ undercoordination, halide vacancies) and forming robust low-dimensional surface structures, guided by density functional theory (DFT) screening.
3. **Construct graded 2D/3D heterostructure architectures** that combine the high efficiency of 3D perovskites with the moisture resistance and ion-migration suppression of 2D perovskites, optimized for both n-i-p and p-i-n device configurations.
4. **Achieve PSC devices with PCE above 25% and T80 lifetime above 5,000 hours** under continuous one-sun illumination at maximum power point (MPP), together with damp-heat stability in accordance with IEC 61215 protocols.
5. **Elucidate degradation mechanisms** through operando and in-situ characterization, linking microscopic defect evolution to macroscopic performance loss.

## 4. Literature Review

### 4.1 Defects and Their Role in Perovskite Degradation

First-principles studies established that the dominant intrinsic point defects in MAPbI3 — iodide interstitials and lead vacancies — introduce shallow levels, underpinning the celebrated "defect tolerance" of LHPs. However, this tolerance is compromised at surfaces and GBs, where undercoordinated Pb2+ and Pb–I antisite defects create deep traps. Deep-level transient spectroscopy (DLTS) and admittance spectroscopy have quantified trap densities of 10^15–10^16 cm^-3 in polycrystalline films, predominantly localized at GBs. These defects not only limit Voc but also catalyse decomposition: undercoordinated Pb2+ reacts readily with oxygen and moisture, while iodide vacancies facilitate ion migration under field and illumination, which in turn accelerates both phase segregation and interfacial reactions.

### 4.2 Passivation Strategies

Passivation approaches can be broadly classified into three categories. *Lewis base passivation* exploits electron-donating groups (e.g., thiophene, pyridine, carbonyl) to coordinate with undercoordinated Pb2+. *Ionic passivation* uses organic ammonium salts (e.g., phenethylammonium, butylammonium) to form 2D Ruddlesden–Popper phases that physically shield the 3D surface and suppress ion migration. *Multifunctional passivation* combines multiple anchoring groups within a single molecule to address several defect types simultaneously — theophylline, caffeine, and ionic liquids have all shown enhanced Voc and stability.

Recent work has demonstrated that rational molecular design — incorporating both halide-bonding and hydrogen-bonding motifs — can yield passivation layers with superior thermal and photochemical robustness. Yet, a systematic design principle connecting molecular structure to long-term stability remains underdeveloped, and most reported passivators are optimized for initial efficiency rather than operational lifetime.

### 4.3 Interface Modification and Charge Transport

The perovskite/CTL interface is a critical locus of both recombination and degradation. In n-i-p architectures, the SnO2/perovskite interface suffers from oxygen-vacancy-mediated trap formation and UV instability, while the perovskite/spiro-OMeTAD interface is compromised by LiTFSI migration and dopant oxidation. In p-i-n architectures, NiOx/perovskite interfaces exhibit Ni3+-related oxidative degradation. Interface modifiers — including self-assembled monolayers (SAMs) such as 2PACz and Me-4PACz, inorganic interlayers (e.g., atomic-layer-deposited Al2O3, SnOx), and polyelectrolytes — have been shown to reduce interfacial recombination and improve stability. SAM-based hole-selective contacts, in particular, have enabled PSCs exceeding 25% PCE with substantially improved operational lifetimes.

### 4.4 Stability Testing and Protocols

The community has converged on the ISOS (International Summit on Organic PV Stability) protocols to standardize stability reporting. Key metrics include T80 (time to 80% of initial efficiency) under continuous illumination at MPP (ISOS-L-2), thermal cycling (ISOS-T-1), and damp-heat exposure at 85 °C/85% relative humidity (IEC 61215). State-of-the-art passivated devices now report T80 values exceeding 1,000–3,000 hours, with a few reports surpassing 5,000 hours, though reproducibility and reporting rigour remain ongoing concerns.

### 4.5 Research Gap

Despite substantial progress, three gaps persist: (i) most passivation studies optimize for initial efficiency rather than long-term stability; (ii) the synergistic interplay between defect passivation and interfacial charge extraction is rarely co-engineered; and (iii) operando mechanistic insight linking molecular-level defect dynamics to device-level degradation is limited. This proposal directly addresses these gaps by unifying molecular design, interface engineering, and operando characterization within a single programme.

## 5. Research Methodology

### 5.1 Defect Characterization

The initial phase will establish a quantitative baseline of defect populations and their spatial distribution. **Deep-level transient spectroscopy (DLTS)** and **thermal admittance spectroscopy (TAS)** will resolve trap energy levels and densities within complete devices, with selective measurement of the buried perovskite/CTL interface via hole-only and electron-only device architectures. **Kelvin probe force microscopy (KPFM)** and **conductive AFM (c-AFM)** will map surface potential and current distributions across grain boundaries, while **time-of-flight secondary ion mass spectrometry (TOF-SIMS)** will profile ion migration (I−, Cs+, FA+) across the device stack under bias and illumination stress. **X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS)** and **hard X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (HAXPES)** will probe chemical states at buried interfaces.

### 5.2 Passivation Molecule Design

A library of multifunctional organic molecules will be designed and synthesized, each incorporating multiple anchoring motifs: (a) a Lewis base moiety (e.g., pyridine, imidazole, carbonyl) to coordinate undercoordinated Pb2+; (b) a hydrogen-bond donor/acceptor (e.g., amide, hydroxyl) to interact with halide species; and (c) a steric bulk component (e.g., phenethyl, naphthylmethyl) to promote 2D Ruddlesden–Popper phase formation. Candidate molecules will be pre-screened using **density functional theory (DFT)** to compute binding energies to PbI2-terminated surfaces, defect passivation energies, and projected 2D formation energies. Promising candidates — anticipated to include theophylline derivatives, phosphonium salts, and bifunctional ammonium cations — will be synthesized in-house and applied via anti-solvent dripping or post-deposition spin-coating. A central hypothesis is that molecules combining multiple, spatially separated anchoring groups will exhibit both stronger binding and greater photostability than single-functional analogues.

### 5.3 2D/3D Heterostructure Construction

Graded 2D/3D heterostructures will be fabricated by depositing selected bulky organic ammonium salts atop MA-free mixed-cation perovskite (e.g., FA0.88Cs0.12PbI3) films. Process parameters — salt concentration, solvent polarity, annealing temperature — will be optimized via **grazing-incidence wide-angle X-ray scattering (GIWAXS)** and **scanning electron microscopy (SEM)** to confirm 2D phase formation and preservation of 3D crystallinity. The dimensionality gradient will be tuned (n = 1–5 in (R)2(FA)n−1PbnI3n+1) to balance moisture resistance against charge transport. Both n-i-p (SnO2/perovskite/spiro-OMeTAD) and p-i-n (SAM/perovskite/C60) architectures will be evaluated to ensure generality of the approach.

### 5.4 Device Fabrication and Interface Co-Engineering

Devices will be fabricated on ITO-coated glass using established protocols, with concurrent optimization of CTL interfaces. For p-i-n architectures, SAM hole-selective layers (Me-4PACz, MeO-2PACz) and surface-modified SnO2 will be paired with the optimized passivation chemistry. The objective is to co-engineer the perovskite/CTL energetic alignment (measured by ultraviolet photoelectron spectroscopy, UPS) with defect passivation, minimizing interfacial recombination (quantified by transient photovoltage/photocurrent, TPV/TPC) while maximizing extraction efficiency. This co-engineering is essential because a passivation layer that suppresses recombination but introduces an energetic barrier will ultimately limit fill factor and stability.

### 5.5 Stability Testing

Stability will be evaluated under multiple ISOS protocols: **ISOS-L-2** (continuous 1-sun illumination at MPP, ~65 °C, in both N2 and air), **ISOS-D-1** (dark storage), **ISOS-T-1** (thermal cycling −40 to 85 °C), and **IEC 61215 damp-heat** (85 °C/85% RH). T80 and T95 metrics will be tracked, with a target T80 above 5,000 h under ISOS-L-2. Stability testing will be replicated across at least five devices per condition to ensure statistical rigour, and full testing curves will be reported rather than selective data points.

### 5.6 Operando Mechanistic Studies

To link defect dynamics to degradation, **in-situ GIWAXS** and **in-situ photoluminescence (PL)** will monitor structural and optoelectronic evolution during light soaking. **Operando KPFM** will track surface potential shifts indicative of ion accumulation, while **periodic DLTS/TAS** at defined stress intervals will quantify trap density evolution. Correlative analysis linking microscopic defect signatures to macroscopic efficiency loss will yield a predictive degradation model that connects molecular-level passivation design directly to device lifetime.

## 6. Expected Outcomes

The proposed research is expected to deliver:

- **High-performance, durable PSCs**: PCE above 25% with T80 above 5,000 h under continuous one-sun MPP operation, alongside IEC 61215 damp-heat compliance.
- **A library of multifunctional passivation molecules** with established structure–property relationships linking molecular design to defect passivation efficacy and stability.
- **A mechanistic framework** connecting defect chemistry at buried interfaces to device-level degradation, validated by operando characterization.
- **A scalable 2D/3D heterostructure platform** applicable across n-i-p and p-i-n architectures and compatible with ambient fabrication routes.

These outcomes will represent a meaningful advance toward commercial deployment of perovskite photovoltaics and will contribute fundamental understanding to the broader field of defect-tolerant semiconductors.

## 7. Research Timeline

The PhD programme is planned over four years:

- **Year 1 (Months 1–12)**: Comprehensive literature review; establish baseline MA-free PSC devices; develop defect characterization protocols (DLTS, TAS, KPFM); initial DFT screening of candidate passivation molecules.
- **Year 2 (Months 13–24)**: Synthesize and screen passivation molecules; optimize 2D/3D heterostructure fabrication; demonstrate initial efficiency and stability gains; publish first findings.
- **Year 3 (Months 25–36)**: Co-engineer perovskite/CTL interfaces; conduct operando mechanistic studies; perform extended ISOS stability testing; publish mechanistic work.
- **Year 4 (Months 37–48)**: Validate reproducibility across device architectures; finalize predictive degradation model; thesis writing and dissemination at international conferences.
